Environmental noise screening tool
This tool is currently under construction. Features and data may be incomplete or subject to change.
A screening tool to predict noise levels at nearby receivers. Select source and receiver locations on the map, enter noise source data, and the tool will indicate potential compliance. Depending on location, the criteria will be automatically calculated or can be entered manually.

Notes on MBS 010 screening methodology

Triggering: MBS 010 applies if the site is within an ANEF contour (Aircraft Noise Exposure Forecast) or a Noise Attenuation Area (Noise and Air Emissions Overlay) as identified in the Planning and Design Code. Overlay data is queried from SAPPA (location.sa.gov.au). If neither overlay applies, designated road/rail/mixed use rows show N/A.

Distances to roads: Measured as the straight-line distance from the nearest point on the site boundary (cadastral parcel) to the nearest edge of the designated road corridor polygon, plus 3 m. Per MBS 010, separation distance for roads is measured from a point 3 m inside the road corridor boundary (i.e. 3 m from the corridor edge to the road surface).

Distances to rail: Measured as the straight-line distance from the nearest point on the site boundary to the closest rail line (centreline). Per MBS 010, separation distance for rail is measured from the closest rail line, not the corridor boundary.

Road polygons: Designated Road Polygon data (Type A, B, R) is sourced from SAPPA (Planning & Building > Planning Reference > Designated Road Polygon). Using local GeoJSON files (road_type_a/b/r.geojson). Check SAPPA for confirmation of coverage.

Road names: Road names are sourced from the SA Department for Infrastructure and Transport Speed Zones MapServer (layer 12). The nearest road segment within 200 m of the closest road corridor point is identified and its RDNAME attribute is used.

Road speeds: Posted speed limits are queried from the SA DIT Speed Zones MapServer at the point on the road corridor nearest to the site. If the road name and speed cannot be automatically identified (marked with *), an assumed speed of 50–60 km/h is used for SEC calculation and should be confirmed by the user against actual posted speed limits.

SEC calculation: Sound Exposure Categories are determined per MBS 010 Tables 4.1 (Type A & B roads), 4.2 (Type R roads), and 4.3 (rail). Distance thresholds vary by road type and speed band (50–60, 70–90, 100–110 km/h). For ANR contours, SEC is per Table 4.4 based on the ANR dB(A) value. Mixed land use areas are assigned SEC 1 per Clause 4.3.3 where the zone includes non-residential land uses.

Rail data: Train and tram line data is sourced from SA government shapefiles (centreline geometry).

ANR contours: Aircraft Noise Reduction contours are sourced from local GeoJSON data (Adelaide, Parafield, RAAF Edinburgh airports).

Limitations: Distances are straight-line only and do not account for terrain, barriers, or building shielding. All values are indicative — verify with SAPPA and consult an acoustic engineer for detailed assessment. The governing SEC for a building element is the highest SEC from all applicable sound sources (Clause 4.2.2).

Propagation method

Select the sound propagation model. The simple method uses geometric spreading only (−20 log10 d − 8 dB). ISO 9613-2 adds frequency-dependent atmospheric absorption (ISO 9613-1) and ground attenuation per octave band (§7.3.1 detailed method with source, receiver, and middle region contributions).
Source heights use library defaults and can be adjusted per source row when visible. Lmax predictions always use the simple method.
Notes on ISO 9613-2 implementation

Standard: Acoustics — Attenuation of sound during propagation outdoors — Part 2: General method of calculation (ISO 9613-2:1996).

Overall formula: For each octave band f (63 Hz to 8 kHz):
Lp(f) = Lw(f) − A(f),   where   A(f) = Adiv + Aatm(f) + Agr(f)

Geometric divergence (Adiv): Point source, free-field spherical spreading per Equation 8:
Adiv = 20 log10(d) + 11  dB

Atmospheric absorption (Aatm): Per octave band, calculated from ISO 9613-1 using temperature and humidity:
Aatm(f) = α(f) × d / 1000  dB
where α(f) is the absorption coefficient in dB/km. Default values at 10 °C, 70% RH: [0.1, 0.4, 1.0, 1.9, 3.7, 9.7, 32.8, 117.0] dB/km for [63, 125, 250, 500, 1k, 2k, 4k, 8k] Hz. For other conditions, the full ISO 9613-1 formula is used (O2 and N2 relaxation frequencies, saturation pressure, molar concentration).

Ground attenuation (Agr): Frequency-dependent, detailed method per §7.3.1, Table 3:
Agr(f) = As(f) + Ar(f) + Am(f)
Source region As and receiver region Ar use height-dependent functions a′(h), b′(h), c′(h), d′(h) for 125–1000 Hz bands, scaled by ground factor G. At 63 Hz, As = Ar = −1.5 dB (ground reflection). At 2k–8k Hz, As = −1.5(1−G).
Middle region Am applies when d > 30(hs+hr), with factor q = 1 − 30(hs+hr)/d.
Negative Agr values represent ground reflection amplification (e.g. hard paved surfaces). On soft porous ground (G=1), Agr can exceed 10 dB at 250–500 Hz (ground dip effect).

Ground factor G: G=0 for hard reflective surfaces (concrete, asphalt, water); G=0.5 for mixed; G=1 for soft porous ground (grass, soil, rural land). A single G value is used for all three regions in this tool.

Band summation: The overall A-weighted predicted level is the energetic sum across all octave bands:
LAeq = 10 log10 Σ 10Lp(f)/10  dB(A)
Source spectra in the library are pre-A-weighted, so no additional A-weighting correction is applied.

Multi-source combination: Individual source predictions are combined energetically (Equation 3). Each source’s level is adjusted for quantity (10 log10 n), operational time fraction (10 log10(t/T)), and barrier (−5 dB if selected).

Barrier attenuation: A simplified fixed −5 dB broadband reduction is applied when barrier is set to “Yes”. The standard’s detailed barrier calculation (Abar via Fresnel number) is not implemented — actual barrier performance varies by frequency and geometry.

Directivity (Dc): All sources are treated as omnidirectional (Dc = 0). Source directivity is not modelled.

Screening (Abar), miscellaneous (Amisc): Not implemented. No terrain screening, industrial-site screening, or housing/vegetation attenuation is included. Terrain is assumed flat.

Convergence with simple method: On hard ground (G=0) with no atmospheric absorption, Agr = −3 dB at all bands, so the ISO formula reduces to Lw − 20 log10(d) − 8, matching the simple hemispherical spreading method.

Limitations: This is a screening-level implementation. For detailed assessment, use dedicated acoustic modelling software (e.g. SoundPLAN, CadnaA, iNoise) with site-specific terrain, barriers, building reflections, and meteorological corrections (Cmet).

Characteristic penalties

Apply a characteristic penaltyThe presence of an annoying characteristic attracts a decibel penalty under clause 13(3) of the Noise Policy. A penalty is applied because noise with such dominant characteristics are more noticeable and annoying when compared to a constant, broadband or steady noise. For a penalty to be applied, the Noise Policy requires the characteristic to be fundamental to the nature and impact of the noise. The characteristic should dominate the overall noise impact, rather than simply be part of it. Noise characteristics include tonal, impulsive, low frequency, intermittent and modulating. Sound pressure level calculator
This calculator provides an indication of the sound pressure level at a specified distance due to various noise sources. It does not consider ground and air absorption or meteorological effects, which are more significant over large distances. It is intended to be used a simple screening tool to determine whether more accurate noise modelling and advice should be provided by an acoustic consultant.
Barrier attenuation
The barrier provides overall 5 dB attenuation. This is a conservative indication of the attenuation provided by a solid acoustic barrier which blocks line of sight between the noise source and receiver. Actual acoustic barrier attenuation may vary in complex terrain or environments with hard reflective surfaces in proximity to the source and/or receiver.
Operational time
The calculation provides an option to input the expected operational time during a 15 minute assessment period. This will provide a 10·log time weighting to the source level to account for the contribution during the assessment period.
